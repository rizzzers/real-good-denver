Developing Denver

A massive RiNo transit village just broke ground - LINK 
Formativ Real Estate Group officially broke ground on Rowan, a 358-unit apartment community at 3875 Walnut Street, the latest phase of a multi-building transit-oriented development at 38th and Blake that will eventually deliver more than 650 housing units, a hotel, and retail steps from the A-Line.

Littleton bets $2M on a Peyton Manning restaurant complex - LINK 
The Littleton City Council voted 4-3 to approve a public-private partnership for First Street Farms, a $28 million development linked to former Broncos quarterback Peyton Manning that will include a 15,000-square-foot restaurant, an indoor event venue, trails, and free outdoor programming along the South Platte River corridor.

Denver is using marijuana tax money to back small businesses - LINK 
The Denver City Council approved a three-year, $9 million low-interest loan program for undercapitalized businesses, drawing funds from a retail marijuana tax account honoring the late National Black Chamber of Commerce chair Herman Malone, with applications and info sessions open now.

The city is eyeing a massive housing overhaul near an RTD station - LINK 
The Denver Housing Authority is exploring a large-scale redevelopment of the Westridge and Mulroy properties near the Knox RTD Station, a move that signals a push to leverage transit-adjacent land for density and affordable housing.

Downtown Denver's office vacancy is quietly becoming an investor gold mine - LINK 
With some of the highest office vacancy rates in the country and dozens of buildings in or facing foreclosure, downtown Denver's distressed commercial real estate market is drawing opportunistic investors eyeing empty towers for residential and mixed-use conversions.

 

Denver News

Denver's surveillance cameras went dark for a month, and car theft dropped anyway - LINK 
After the city's Flock Safety license plate reader contract expired in March, Denver had zero automated cameras for the entire month of April, yet auto theft still fell 29 percent compared to the same month a year ago, raising uncomfortable questions about what the $666,000-a-year system was actually buying.

Colorado busted a drug ring selling MDMA and ketamine disguised as luxury candy - LINK 
The "Molly Wonka" investigation ended in a 48-count grand jury indictment against seven people accused of running a two-year operation out of Basalt that packaged narcotics into branded chocolate bars and nasal sprays sold under names like "Renaissance" and "Alpen Glow."

Governor Polis declared a statewide drought emergency as Colorado's driest year on record gets worse - LINK 
With 93 percent of the state in moderate to exceptional drought, snowpack at its lowest level since 1987, and lakes sitting 25 feet below normal, Polis activated Phase 3 of Colorado's Drought Response Plan and is now positioned to request a federal disaster declaration.

Denver told residents to start catching stray dogs themselves - LINK 
Denver Animal Protection is down to just 11 officers covering a city of 740,000 people after budget cuts eliminated positions, so the city formally asked residents to help secure loose dogs while officers focus on cruelty and neglect calls.

El Nino is officially here and it could be the strongest on record - LINK 
NOAA officially declared El Nino conditions on June 11, with a 63 percent chance this event becomes one of the most powerful since 1950, which could bring a snowier winter to southern Colorado but offers little immediate relief for the drought-stricken summer ahead.

No Kings protesters are organizing across Colorado this week - LINK 
Pro-democracy demonstrators tied to the national No Kings movement are planning community-level gatherings across Colorado, with around 330 events scheduled nationwide as organizers shift from downtown marches to neighborhood-based action.

Denver's tornado sirens went off by accident during an actual tornado watch - LINK 
On June 8, tornado sirens blared across Denver in what officials later confirmed was an inadvertent activation, a week after golf ball-sized hail had already pounded parts of the city and put residents freshly on edge about severe weather.

Denver Pride is leaving Civic Center Park for the first time in its history - LINK 
The annual festival that draws 550,000 people is ditching its longtime home for a June 28 takeover of 16th Street from Broadway to Arapahoe, with a new location, a new logo, and a full month of activation events across the city leading up to it.

Blake Street Tavern is finally coming back, three years later and under a new name - LINK 
After sitting dark next to Coors Field since 2023, the iconic sports bar returns June 20 as Tavern on Blake Street under new ownership, with an arcade, live music venue, and a stated mission to be a place for everybody again.

Downtown Denver's commercial real estate market is in a deeper crisis than most realize - LINK 
A new investigation found the central business district has some of the highest office vacancy rates in the country, with dozens of buildings in or nearing foreclosure, even as investors begin circling the distress for residential conversion plays.
